(01 · Three truths)

The problem

Three sentences. If any is wrong, the premise breaks.

1

Local merchants spend billions on ad networks with no proof of store visits

Instagram, Yelp, Google, and Mailchimp take tens of billions per year from local SMBs. Not one dollar is tied to a verified store visit. They cannot measure it.

2

Creators post about local merchants daily but are never paid because attribution cannot be proven

The $250B creator economy is global. But local creators—3K to 100K followers—have zero infrastructure to monetize local impact. They post, merchants don't pay, everyone loses.

3

Consumers are force-fed algorithm-ranked ads instead of trusted tastemakers

Meta and Google optimize for engagement, not relevance. Consumers see ads from brands they don't trust. The tastemakers they actually follow get no revenue from their influence.

(02 · Four structural windows)

Why now: 4 forces

Each opened in the past five years. Together they unlock a $15-40B market.

Force 1 · Cookie attribution is dead

iOS 14.5 (2021) + Chrome 3P cookie deprecation (2024). Digital marketers can no longer prove attribution at the ad-network level. This is not a weakness of digital—it is a permanent structural shift. Only offline (physical verification) now works.

Force 2 · No creator infrastructure for local impact

The creator economy is platform-dependent (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube). All revenue flows through their algorithms. No independent creator can monetize local impact. This is a vacuum waiting to fill.

Force 3 · FTC 16 CFR Part 255 actively enforced

Merchants who pay creators for merchant-related content must provide verified, disclosed, decaying attribution records. Legacy platforms do not. We do. Compliance is now a competitive advantage.

Force 4 · QR is post-COVID native

In 2019, QR codes were fringe. In 2026, they are normal—receipts, storefronts, packaging. No education required. A customer scanning is frictionless.

(03 · Six steps: how it works)

The mechanism

A creator posts. A customer scans. A merchant pays. Everything verified.

  1. Step 1
    Creator applies
    Verified on 12 dimensions: follower count, audience locality, past merchant engagement, content quality, disclosure hygiene.
  2. Step 2
    Merchant posts campaign
    Create: target neighborhood, budget ($200-2k typical), success metric (10-50 verified customers). Zero upfront cost in pilot phase.
  3. Step 3
    Push assigns creator
    Algorithm matches: creator locality overlap + audience relevance + past ROAS + creator availability. Primary + standby creator (see moat §2).
  4. Step 4
    Creator posts with unique QR
    Instagram, TikTok, Xiaohongshu: creator discloses 'Paid partnership with [Merchant] via Push.' Unique QR in post links to merchant landing. Attribution tracking starts.
  5. Step 5
    Customer scans → visits
    QR scan creates event record: device fingerprint + IP + timestamp + creator ID + merchant ID. Customer visits store within 24-72h window. Merchant confirms via POS or staff.
  6. Step 6
    Push pays both parties
    Merchant: charged $40 per verified customer (deferred from upfront cost). Creator: $24 per verified customer (60% margin; tied to tier and reputation). Both parties get disclosed, immutable audit trail (FTC 16 CFR Part 255 compliant).
(04 · Why we can't be copied)

Three moats

Not a technology moat. Not a brand moat. Structural + network + regulatory.

M1 · Neighborhood Attribution Graph

We own verified-customer data at the creator–merchant–neighborhood level. 40K records by Year 1 end (beachhead). Competitors entering a new neighborhood start at zero. Our graph compounds: higher creator scores, better merchant pricing, faster cold-start matching. Non-exportable.

M2 · Creator Reputation Lock

Earnings and tier are non-portable. A creator leaving takes zero reputation with them. Career capital is locked to Push. Portability is our killer friction.

M3 · FTC-Compliant Attribution Records

We generate what FTC 16 CFR Part 255 requires: verified disclosure, decaying attribution window, immutable audit trail. Merchants who work with us have legal cover. Legacy agencies + platforms do not. Compliance is table stakes—and an unfair advantage against peers.

(08 · What can go wrong)

Risks stated honestly

We don't hide downside scenarios. We state them clearly.

Risk 1 · Phase 0 misses

If we hit the kill criteria (W1 <5 convos, W2 0 LOI, W3 3+ failures, W4 <3 verified transactions), we pause. We do not push forward into Phase 1. This is truth.

Risk 2 · Meta/Google free attribution tool (Medium, Y2–3)

Both have enough data to build verified-transaction tracking. If they launch it for free, they own the margin floor. Upside: we'd be acquired at scale before it lands. Downside: we have 18–24 months of runway before competitive pressure.

Risk 3 · FTC guidance change (Low, any year)

If 16 CFR Part 255 is reinterpreted to narrow disclosure or attribution windows, we adapt or shift. Current enforcement is active, which is our tail wind now. Reversal is unlikely but not impossible.
