# PUSH — Master Strategy Skill

## Purpose
This document defines the strategic foundation of Push as a company, not merely a product. It clarifies what Push is, what it is not, and how future decisions should be evaluated.

---

## 1. Strategic Definition

**Push is a local creator performance infrastructure layer that helps merchants acquire customers through measurable creator-driven demand.**

Push is **not**:
- a generic influencer marketplace
- a local discovery app
- a content community
- a reward app
- a lightweight loyalty system

Push is:
- a transaction system
- a trust system
- a creator credit system
- a local demand orchestration system

The strategic test for any feature is simple:

> Does this increase measurable customer acquisition, trust, repeatability, or platform defensibility?

If not, it should not be prioritized.

---

## 2. Core Problem

### Merchant Problem
Local merchants do not reliably know:
- which creators will actually drive new customers
- how to compare creators beyond followers and aesthetics
- how to run structured campaigns repeatedly
- how to avoid wasting money on low-performing creators

### Creator Problem
Rising creators do not reliably have:
- pricing power
- a verifiable performance record
- upward mobility
- a stable path from free samples to paid work

### Market Problem
The creator economy has abundant supply, but the supply is not structured into a repeatable acquisition channel for local commerce.

---

## 3. Core Insight

Creators are not just media inventory.  
They are **unstructured customer acquisition channels**.

That means the real opportunity is not “help creators collaborate with merchants.”  
The real opportunity is to **standardize, verify, rank, and route creator-driven demand**.

This is the difference between a marketplace and infrastructure.

---

## 4. What Makes Push Non-Cliché

Push avoids being derivative by refusing to define itself as:
- “Yelp for influencers”
- “Uber for local creator marketing”
- “MileagePlus X for creators”
- “creator loyalty platform”

Those analogies may help internal ideation, but they weaken external positioning.

The correct external framing is:

> Push lets local businesses buy customer acquisition from rising creators through verified fulfillment and conversion history.

That is substantially stronger because it centers:
- merchant outcomes
- measurement
- structured supply
- repeatability

---

## 5. Strategic Pillars

### Pillar 1: Offer OS
Merchants should define growth goals, not write vague collaboration posts.

The system should convert merchant intent into structured campaigns such as:
- launch a new item
- drive off-peak traffic
- fill early event attendance
- increase first-time visits
- improve weekday demand
- test a menu item or experience

### Pillar 2: Creator Credit
Creators should not be valued mainly by followers.

They should be valued by:
- completion rate
- reliability
- merchant satisfaction
- repeat booking likelihood
- dispute history
- geographic fit
- category fit

### Pillar 3: Attribution
Push must verify whether a campaign actually produced meaningful value.

Attribution can include:
- unique offers or codes
- check-in and timestamp proof
- merchant confirmation
- proof-of-visit artifacts
- content verification
- redemption evidence
- CRM or POS integrations later

### Pillar 4: Demand Surface
The map is not a decorative browsing layer.
It should function as a real-time demand interface:
- where opportunities are
- when they close
- who qualifies
- what they pay
- how scarce they are

### Pillar 5: Liquidity Layer
The standby mechanism is strategically important because it increases:
- slot fill rate
- creator engagement
- urgency
- merchant confidence
- time-sensitive campaign efficiency

---

## 6. Company-Building Thesis

If Push succeeds, it becomes the system that:
- standardizes local creator acquisition
- ranks creators by commercial performance
- routes merchants toward better outcomes
- compounds proprietary creator performance data
- creates lock-in through access, trust, and workflow

The end state is not “a successful marketplace.”
The end state is a **new market layer** for local creator-driven demand.

---

## 7. What to Prioritize

### Highest Priority
- verifiable campaign outcomes
- creator scoring architecture
- repeat merchant workflow
- creator retention through earning progression
- anti-disintermediation design
- standby and time-sensitive opportunity mechanics

### Medium Priority
- premium membership logic
- advanced analytics
- vertical-specific templates
- quality-of-life automation

### Low Priority
- social/community features
- broad creator expression tools
- ornamental loyalty features without economic purpose

---

## 8. Strategic Red Flags

The company becomes strategically weak if it starts to behave like:
- a sample-giveaway app
- a creator coupon engine
- a posting board for merchants
- a local discovery map with weak economics
- a content engagement platform with shallow verification

If those signals appear, the company is drifting away from infrastructure and toward commodity territory.

---

## 9. Final Strategic Standard

Every major decision should answer at least one of these questions positively:

1. Does this improve merchant ROI clarity?
2. Does this improve creator earning power or quality sorting?
3. Does this deepen platform defensibility?
4. Does this reduce fraud, operational friction, or repeat campaign cost?
5. Does this make future transactions more likely to stay on-platform?

If the answer is no, the idea is likely noise.
