Investor Deep Dive

15 hard questions. Direct answers.

The questions every investor asks about Push — from business model to moat to risk. No dodging.

I · Core Questions
Q1
Why won't this become another generic marketplace?
Investors worry: all two-sided platforms end up with low quality + price wars.

Push is not an open marketplace — it is an attribution infrastructure layer. We don't let merchants post freely or creators apply randomly. The QR code is bound to a specific creator × merchant × campaign combination. Every payout requires verified conversion proof. The system is closed by design: you can't fake a scan without a physical presence at the register.

Q2
Why won't merchants bypass you?
Investors worry: merchants and creators will work together directly.

Three lock-in layers. 1. Attribution records are non-portable — your verified conversion history lives on Push. Leave and you start from zero. 2. FTC compliance records — Push generates the verified, disclosed, decaying attribution records that FTC 16 CFR Part 255 requires. Merchants can't easily self-build this. 3. Stripe Connect structure — we're embedded in the payout flow. Bypassing us means rebuilding the settlement layer from scratch.

Q3
How do you solve cold start?
Investors worry: two-sided markets are the hardest to bootstrap.

We don't start as a marketplace. We start with 5–7 warm merchant relationships in Williamsburg that our ops lead already has. Hand-picked creators, not open registration. Founder-led onboarding. Phase 0 goal is not scale — it's 10 verified attribution transactions and 80% 90-day retention. Prove the unit before building the machine.

Q4
How do you ensure creator quality?
Investors worry: bad supply will destroy the platform.

Quality is enforced through verified output, not moderation. Creators earn tier status through completed, verified conversions — not follower count. Six tiers: Community → Explorer → Maker → Studio → Partner → Icon. Underperformers lose tier access and campaign eligibility. The scoring is transparent, so creators know exactly what they need to do to move up — or to avoid falling down.

Q5
How do you handle attribution?
Investors worry: how do you prove a creator actually brought customers?

Three independent signals per conversion: QR scan + receipt timestamp + merchant confirmation. We record one unified attribution_event row per verified visit. We're honest about what we can and can't prove: we don't claim 100% precision, but we provide far more verifiable proof than any existing alternative (which is usually zero). Merchants care about one question: should I do this again? We answer it.

II · Business & Economics
Q6
How do you make money?
Investors worry: how do you monetize without crushing creator margins or merchant ROI?

Three tiers. Attribution Lite — free acquisition loop, Push earns from verified conversion rev share only. Essentials — $99/mo, always-on attribution + campaign analytics. Pro Year 1 — 5% of attributed revenue, cap $179/mo, floor $49/mo. Outcome-based: merchants only pay in months Push drives verified revenue. Year 2+ auto-converts to $199 flat or $349 Advanced. Push never holds money — all payouts through Stripe Connect.

Q7
Does unit economics work?
Investors worry: can you actually make money per transaction?

Per verified conversion on Pro Year 1: merchant pays 5% of attributed revenue (capped at $179/mo). Creator payout scales by tier (Community → Studio). Push net take is the spread between merchant payment and creator payout plus Stripe fees. Blended gross margin target: 55% at scale. The leverage is in the conversion volume: more verified conversions per merchant per month = more attributed revenue = more Push earnings, with near-zero marginal cost on the verification side (AI-driven).

Q8
How do you define PMF?
Investors worry: how do you know this actually resonates?

When merchants re-book campaigns without being pushed. Three signals: repeat rate > 80%, campaign re-booking within 30 days of completion, and ROAS lift > 25% vs their baseline. Phase 0 is designed to test exactly these three signals in a controlled environment before Phase 1 expansion.

III · Market & Competition
Q9
Why now?
Investors worry: what if this is too early or already too late?

Four forces converged in 2026. 1. Cookie attribution is dead — iOS 14.5 (2021) + Chrome third-party cookie deprecation (2024). The old rail is gone. 2. Creator economy has no local rail — $250B globally, but local creators (3K–100K followers) have zero infrastructure to get paid for local impact. 3. FTC 16 CFR Part 255 is actively enforced — merchants need verified, disclosed attribution records. Legacy platforms don't provide them. 4. QR is post-COVID native — scanning at checkout no longer needs education.

Q10
Who are your competitors?
Investors worry: aren't there already players in this space?

No direct attribution-rail competitors exist for local commerce. Current alternatives: DM creators directly (no attribution), hire agencies (expensive, no verification), run TikTok/Yelp/Google ads (impression-based, no store-visit proof). The key difference: they help you spend money on creators. We prove which spending drove physical customers. That's a different product.

Q11
Why won't big platforms do this?
Investors worry: can't Meta or TikTok just copy you?

Because physical-visit attribution is operationally hard in ways that don't scale the way big platforms want to scale. Every neighborhood is different. Merchant onboarding is high-touch. Creator verification requires local knowledge. QR placement and staff training vary by store. Big platforms optimize for horizontal reach. We optimize for vertical depth in specific neighborhoods. The moat is local density, not global distribution.

IV · Moat & Defensibility
Q12
What is your moat?
Investors worry: what keeps someone from cloning this?

Three compounding moats. M1 — Neighborhood Attribution Graph: every verified conversion is a training signal. After 1,000 events per neighborhood, matching accuracy becomes irreproducible. M2 — Creator Reputation Lock: verified conversion history is non-portable. Leaving Push = starting from zero. M3 — FTC Compliance Records: we generate the attribution records the law requires. This is a structural moat that becomes more valuable as enforcement tightens.

Q13
Why won't it devolve into a low-quality market?
Investors worry: won't bad creators undercut good ones?

Because every payout requires a verified physical conversion, not a posted piece of content. You can't fake a QR scan + receipt timestamp + merchant confirmation combination. The verification stack prevents quality decay at the source — low-quality creators simply don't generate verified conversions, and without verified conversions, they don't get paid and eventually leave.

V · Risk & Vision
Q14
What is the biggest risk?
Investors worry: what could kill this company?

Phase 0 is the thesis. If 5–7 Williamsburg coffee shops don't show > 25% ROAS lift and > 80% 90-day retention by August 2026, the Manhattan premium price point is unlikely to hold. We pause, re-scope, and tell investors before they find out. The kill criteria are defined before launch, which is how we prevent silent drift. We state this risk first, not last.

Q15
What are you actually building?
Investors worry: is this a nice feature, or a standalone company?

We are building the attribution rail that connects creator posts to physical in-store purchases — the infrastructure that proves which post drove which visit, so creators get paid for local impact and merchants get measurable ROI on creator spend. Three parties (merchant, creator, consumer) settling on one verified ledger. Stripe Connect underneath. FTC-compliant by design. The rail that local commerce doesn't have yet.
