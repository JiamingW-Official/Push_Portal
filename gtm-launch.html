GTM Experiments & Launch Loops

How Push launches as a concentrated market experiment, not a broad platform announcement.

Density changes behavior for both creators and merchants. The first neighborhood becomes the blueprint for the next one.

Deep dive into Push's go-to-market launch plan. For the investor-facing summary, see Pitch Deck.
PHILOSOPHY

Launch as a Concentrated Experiment

Push should NOT launch as a broad platform. Launch as a concentrated market experiment. The first goal is to prove: merchants repeat, creators progress, campaigns can be fulfilled cleanly, and local density changes behavior.

Expansion without proof of these four things is likely fake progress. Every launch cohort should teach the platform something real — not just fill a user count.

MARKET ENTRY

Wedge Selection Criteria

A strong first wedge reduces operational complexity and maximizes the chance of early proof. Bad wedge selection makes everything harder.

Clear Local Demand
Merchants in this category have an identifiable, recurring problem that better creator campaigns can solve — not just a theoretical use case.
Visual Creator Compatibility
The experience or product creates natural content opportunities — something worth visiting, experiencing, and sharing.
Frequent Consumer Purchase Behavior
Category has repeat purchase cycles — weekly, not annually. Repeat purchase frequency supports merchant campaign repetition.
Manageable Campaign Complexity
Campaign deliverables are clear, proofs are simple, and fulfillment does not require complex logistics or extended time.
Merchant Openness to Experimentation
Owners in this space are willing to try new acquisition models, tolerate some learning-period messiness, and give honest feedback.
Likely Strong Wedges
Cafes & Coffee Dessert Shops Beauty Appointments Boutique Fitness Intros Small Events & Openings

Design Intent

Within this zone: merchants are dense enough that creators can visit multiple in one trip. Standby has real meaning — creators nearby can accept quickly. Map value becomes visible immediately. Each campaign feeds the next one's awareness.

EXPERIMENTS

Four Early GTM Experiments

Each experiment is designed to produce a specific insight — not just usage data. Run in parallel across the first launch cohort.

A
MERCHANT SIDE
Merchant Pain Discovery
Interview and onboard 10 merchants across the launch zone. Present multiple campaign objective types. Track which objectives produce the strongest enthusiasm response. Measure whether merchants care more about traffic, launch awareness, fill rate, or social proof — and whether this varies by category.
GOAL: Which objective resonates TRACK: Enthusiasm by type
B
CREATOR SIDE
Creator Supply Quality Test
Compare three creator segments: students, niche local creators, and vertical-focused creators (beauty/food/events). Track response speed to campaign offers, completion rate, and merchant-rated quality. Determine which segment delivers most value in the wedge categories chosen.
GOAL: Best creator profile TRACK: Speed, completion, quality
C
STANDBY SYSTEM
Standby Utility Test
Create controlled slot release moments — campaigns where a creator drops out and a standby slot opens. Measure fill speed: how quickly a standby creator accepts. Measure conversion to completion: do standby-filled slots complete at the same rate as direct-fill slots?
GOAL: Does standby actually work TRACK: Fill speed & completion
D
MERCHANT REPEAT
Merchant Repeat Trigger Test
After first campaign completion, split merchants into two groups: Group A receives post-campaign performance recommendations and a next-campaign suggestion. Group B receives a standard summary only. Compare 30-day repeat campaign rate between groups. Secondary test: compare reporting depth — detailed vs. summary reporting effects on repeat intent.
GOAL: Repeat drivers TRACK: 30-day repeat rate
GROWTH MECHANICS

Three Launch Loops

Each loop is self-reinforcing. Growth emerges from repeated value, not novelty. The loops must be running before geographic expansion begins.

LOOP 02 · CREATOR PROGRESSION

FAILURE MODES

Four GTM Mistakes to Avoid

These mistakes are common in platform launches and each one derails a different part of the launch loop.

A
Overbroad Geography
Launching across a city or multiple neighborhoods before proving value in one. Wide launch kills density — creators can't fill campaigns efficiently, standby loses meaning, and map value disappears.
B
Overbroad Supply
Attracting too many creators too early — especially unvetted ones — lowers average quality. Merchant trust depends on consistent creator output. One bad campaign can erode a merchant relationship built over weeks.
C
Weak Merchant Education
If merchants don't understand the campaign's purpose, what a good outcome looks like, and what their role is in creator success — results disappoint. Education is not a one-time onboarding step; it's continuous.
D
PR Before Repetition
Generating awareness or press coverage before merchants are repeating and the launch loop is running creates shallow traction. New users arrive to a platform that hasn't proven it works — and churn immediately.
METRICS

03

04

EXPANSION GATES

When to Expand

Geographic or category expansion is only justified when the existing launch zone is demonstrably healthy. Expansion is not a goal — it's a consequence of working loops.

✓
Merchant repeat exists — at least one repeat cohort from the first batch is running new campaigns without active solicitation.
✓
Creator quality remains strong — completion rates have not declined as the cohort size grew. New creators onboarded meet baseline quality standards.
✓
Standby remains meaningful — slots are being filled by standby creators, not just sitting open. Standby response rate within acceptable range.
✓
Operations stay controlled — the current zone is manageable without fires. Disputes are below acceptable threshold. Integrity flags are rare.
✓
Campaign outcome quality is stable — merchant-reported satisfaction scores have not declined. Post-campaign recommendation uptake is positive.
CAUTION
“Expansion without these signals is likely fake progress.”
FINAL STANDARD

Each launch cohort teaches the platform something real. Growth emerges from repeated value, not novelty. Density changes behavior for both creators and merchants.

The first neighborhood becomes a blueprint for the next one. Replicate only what worked — not the entire setup.
