# PUSH — Unit Economics and Revenue Modeling Skill

## Purpose
This document defines how Push should think about revenue quality, cost structure, contribution margin, and the path toward a durable business model.

This is not a pricing brainstorm.
It is a framework for determining whether the company can become financially strong rather than merely operationally busy.

---

## 1. Core Economic Principle

Push should not optimize for activity alone.  
It should optimize for **high-quality, repeatable, margin-positive demand routing**.

A strong marketplace-infrastructure company does not win by maximizing:
- signups
- campaigns created
- map opens
- profile views

It wins by maximizing:
- merchant repeat rate
- creator completion quality
- transaction reliability
- high-confidence attribution
- repeatable contribution margin

---

## 2. Economic Layers of the Business

### Layer 1: Merchant Subscription Revenue
This is the stability layer.

Examples of merchant plan value:
- campaign creation access
- better discovery and routing
- creator quality filtering
- dashboard and reporting
- repeat-campaign automation
- lower operational burden

#### Why It Matters
Subscription revenue provides predictability and reduces dependence on one-off transactions.

---

### Layer 2: Campaign Service Fees
This is the operational monetization layer.

Campaign fees can be justified if Push is providing:
- creator routing
- workflow structure
- proof collection
- settlement and verification
- reporting outputs

#### Why It Matters
This fee ties revenue to merchant action without requiring every campaign to become deeply performance-priced immediately.

---

### Layer 3: Performance-Based Take Rate
This is the strongest long-term monetization layer.

Performance-based monetization can be linked to:
- verified completion
- payout size
- redemption outcome bands
- campaign success events
- merchant-defined result thresholds

#### Why It Matters
Take rate aligns the platform with real value creation, but only becomes strong when attribution quality is strong.

---

### Layer 4: Creator Premium Revenue
This is an optional acceleration layer, not the business foundation.

Creator premium should only exist if it improves:
- earning velocity
- opportunity access
- payout timing
- quality of pipeline

It should not exist as a vanity subscription.

---

### Layer 5: Future Financial Products
Potential future layers:
- payout acceleration
- creator cash advance
- insured settlement
- premium analytics
- chain-level merchant reporting

These should come later, after core transactional trust is proven.

---

## 3. Core Cost Structure

Push will likely carry costs in the following areas:

### Variable Costs
- payment processing
- fraud review
- support intervention
- merchant onboarding support
- creator dispute handling
- verification overhead
- local density activation costs

### Fixed or Semi-Fixed Costs
- engineering
- product development
- infrastructure
- account management
- operational tooling
- growth experimentation

---

## 4. What Good Unit Economics Should Eventually Look Like

The goal is not just “revenue > cost.”
The goal is to create a system where repeat behavior gets progressively cheaper to support and more valuable to the platform.

A healthy mature campaign should show:
- fast fill time
- low fraud review burden
- clear verification path
- limited creator or merchant confusion
- minimal manual intervention
- repeatable merchant likelihood
- creator progression value

---

## 5. Merchant-Side Economic Logic

A merchant will continue using Push when they perceive:
- legible acquisition cost
- superior creator quality vs. ad hoc alternatives
- low setup friction
- strong repeat performance signals
- easier off-peak or strategic goal management

→ See [Doc 01] for the strategic positioning merchants should adopt; see [Doc 03] for detailed merchant acquisition and activation playbook.

The core principle: merchants must perceive Push as more structured, accountable, and repeatable than direct DM outreach or local agencies. Perfect attribution is not required initially, but operational clarity is essential.

---

## 6. Creator-Side Economic Logic

Creators continue using Push when they experience progression:
- improving deal flow quality
- performance-gated access to better campaigns
- more predictable payout timing
- merchant quality filtering
- on-platform earnings that exceed off-platform alternatives

→ See [Doc 03] for detailed creator acquisition, quality tiers, and engagement mechanics; see [Doc 09] for creator progression signals in launch loops.

Core principle: Push must become the place where creator earnings become clearer, more merit-based, and more cumulative than dispersed creator-merchant relationships.

---

## 7. Key Contribution Margin Questions

Push should constantly ask:

1. How much manual work does one campaign require?
2. Which campaign types create the highest operational burden?
3. Which merchant verticals generate the cleanest repeats?
4. Which creator cohorts produce the lowest support load?
5. What percent of campaigns require exception handling?
6. Which workflows reduce human intervention over time?

---

## 8. Dangerous Economic Traps

### Trap A: High Gross Activity, Low Net Value
Many signups and campaigns, but low merchant repeat and heavy manual support.

### Trap B: Premium Revenue Without Real Utility
Charging creators before proving that premium improves their outcomes.

### Trap C: Weak Attribution with Aggressive Performance Pricing
Taking performance-based fees before the system can verify performance reliably.

### Trap D: Excessive Subsidy Dependence
Relying on giveaways and incentives to prop up retention without underlying economics improving.

---

## 9. Economic Priorities by Phase

### Phase 1
Focus on:
- transaction completion
- merchant repeat
- workflow reliability
- clean vertical selection

### Phase 2
Focus on:
- campaign fee consistency
- creator quality differentiation
- support efficiency
- basic dashboard utility

### Phase 3
Focus on:
- better take-rate logic
- premium tier economics
- optional financial products
- multi-location merchant expansion

---

## 10. Final Standard

A strong Push economy is one where:
- every repeated merchant becomes cheaper to serve
- every high-quality creator becomes more valuable to route
- every clean campaign improves future matching and margin quality
- the platform makes more money because the system is better, not because more noise enters it

That is what good revenue modeling should support.
