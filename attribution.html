Attribution & Verification Framework

How Push builds credible attribution confidence without overclaiming certainty.

Deep dive into Push's attribution framework. For the full product overview, see Project Introduction.
Core Philosophy

Not Perfect. Credible.

Push does not need perfect attribution on day one. The objective is to make results more structured, trustworthy, and decision-useful than today's alternatives.

The bar is not scientific certainty. The bar is: more structured, more trustworthy, and more decision-useful than what merchants and creators have today.
What Attribution Must Answer
01 Did the creator actually fulfill the campaign?
02 Did the audience receive the content?
03 Did the campaign create meaningful merchant-side action?
04 Which creators are more commercially effective?
05 Which campaign types are worth repeating?
Verification Architecture

5 Verification Layers

Each layer builds on the previous. Deeper layers require more infrastructure but produce stronger confidence signals for repeat buying decisions.

01
Fulfillment Verification
DID THE CREATOR SHOW UP?

Confirms the creator's physical or digital presence and execution. The baseline without which no other layer is credible.

  • Visit proof
  • Timestamped upload
  • Merchant acknowledgement
  • Draft submission
  • Content link
  • Presence evidence
02
Content Verification
DID THE CONTENT GO LIVE?

Confirms the content was actually published and remained accessible. Prevents submission of draft content as completed work.

  • Live URL check
  • Screenshot evidence
  • Posting timestamp
  • Persistence checks
  • Basic compliance review
03
Engagement Context
DIRECTIONAL ONLY — NOT PRIMARY

Useful for trending analysis and creator comparison, but weak as primary commercial evidence. Engagement data is easy to inflate, hard to correlate with merchant outcomes.

  • Views
  • Clicks
  • Saves
  • Replies
  • Swipe actions
  • Map opens
04
Merchant-Side Action
DID SOMETHING HAPPEN AT THE MERCHANT?

The most commercially meaningful layer. Confirms the campaign moved beyond content publication into actual business outcomes. Requires merchant-side infrastructure.

  • Redemptions
  • Bookings
  • First-time visit markers
  • Offer claims
  • Check-in completions
  • Event attendance confirmation
05
Repeat Signal
THE STRONGEST LONG-TERM SIGNAL

Behavioral evidence that the campaign produced value worth repeating. Self-reinforcing: repeat behavior makes future attribution easier and more confident.

  • Merchant repeat booking of creator
  • Creator invited to higher-value campaign
  • Repeat campaign type success
  • Return merchant behavior
Confidence System

3 Confidence Levels

Every campaign result is assigned a confidence level based on available verification signals. Confidence level determines what decisions the result can support.

Level Signals Present Suitable For Not Suitable For
LOW Content posted, light engagement data, no redemption evidence Creator activity tracking, directional comparison Performance fee justification, repeat booking decisions
MEDIUM Content posted, visit confirmed, claim or intent signal, limited merchant-side action Merchant reporting, general ROI narrative, creator ranking signals High-confidence performance take rates, judicial dispute resolution
HIGH Content posted, fulfillment confirmed, merchant-side redemption or booking evidence, legible campaign outcome Performance-based pricing, repeat booking decisions, premium tier qualification, financial product eligibility N/A — highest available confidence
Campaign-Specific Logic

Attribution by Campaign Type

Different campaign goals require different proof logic. One-size-fits-all measurement is a failure mode, not a feature.

SAMPLING
Product or Experience Sampling
  • Fulfillment completion
  • Content publication
  • Offer claim (if included)
  • Merchant observation record
  • Optional: redemption by audience
EVENT FILL
Event Attendance or Activation
  • RSVP or attendance confirmation
  • Check-in at event
  • Event code usage
  • Time-window correlation
PRODUCT LAUNCH
New Product Awareness Wave
  • Content publication evidence
  • First-wave claims or redemptions
  • Merchant-side awareness signal
  • Repeat demand in short window
OFF-PEAK TRAFFIC
Slow Period Traffic Generation
  • Time-window redemption data
  • Visit counts during targeted periods
  • Repeat merchant perception
Risk Patterns

4 Attribution Failure Modes

Each failure mode appears to be an attribution choice but is actually a trust-destruction mechanism. Avoid at all cost stages.

MODE A Over-Reliance on Views Views are easy to collect but weak as primary commercial signal. Reporting views as success creates merchant expectation misalignment and erodes repeat intent.
MODE B Overclaiming Causality Implying direct causality when only directional evidence exists. When actual outcomes disappoint, the credibility damage is disproportionate to any short-term gain.
MODE C One-Size-Fits-All Measurement Different merchant goals require different proof logic. Applying the same metrics to a product sampling campaign and an off-peak traffic campaign produces misleading results for both.
MODE D Poor Merchant Interpretation Even accurate data fails if merchants can't understand what happened. Dashboards that flood merchants with superficial stats create confusion and undermine repeat confidence.
Design Implications

How Attribution Shapes the Platform

Attribution is not a post-campaign reporting feature. It is a design constraint that shapes how campaigns are created, how creators are briefed, and what the dashboard shows.

  • Define verification method at campaign creation — not retrospectively
  • Match campaign type to appropriate proof logic before launch
  • Expose clear proof steps to creators upfront — no ambiguity about what counts
  • Show confidence level in merchant dashboard alongside results — not just raw numbers
  • Separate vanity indicators from business indicators in all reporting interfaces
  • Maintain audit-friendly milestone logs throughout campaign lifecycle
Reporting Design

What Each Side Sees

Good reporting is not neutral. It converts attribution signals into decision support — for merchants to repeat, and for creators to improve.

Merchant Reporting
REPEAT DECISION SUPPORT
Avoid dashboards that flood merchants with superficial stats. Show what matters for the next booking decision.
  • What was delivered by the creator
  • What was observed on the merchant side
  • How strong the result confidence is
  • How this creator compares to others
  • Whether the campaign is worth repeating
Creator Reporting
PROGRESSION SIGNAL
Attribution converts into progression logic. Converts what was verified into what they need to unlock higher-tier work.
  • What was verified in this campaign
  • How their score changed as a result
  • Which metric category improved
  • What they need to unlock higher-tier work
Final Standard

The Attribution Standard

Attribution quality is the foundation of trust. Trust enables repeat behavior. Repeat behavior generates sustainable economics.

Attribution that increases trust, supports repeat buying decisions, improves creator sorting, resists manipulation, and stays honest about confidence limits. This makes Push more credible than informal creator commerce — and more valuable to every party in the transaction. PUSH — Attribution Framework Standard
