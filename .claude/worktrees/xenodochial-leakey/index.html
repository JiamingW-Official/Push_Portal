Strategic definition, pillars, and company-building thesis

Push IS

✓A transaction system
✓A trust system
✓A creator credit system
✓A local demand orchestration system

Push is NOT

Creators are not just media inventory. They are unstructured customer acquisition channels.
The real opportunity is to standardize, verify, rank, and route creator-driven demand. This is the difference between a marketplace and infrastructure.

STRATEGIC DECISION TEST
Every major decision should answer at least one positively:
1. Does this improve merchant ROI clarity?
2. Does this improve creator earning power or quality sorting?
3. Does this deepen platform defensibility?
4. Does this reduce fraud, friction, or repeat campaign cost?
5. Does this make future transactions more likely to stay on-platform?
If the answer is no, the idea is likely noise.

Consequence: Weak investor conviction, easy dismissal.
Defense: Position as local creator performance infrastructure, not a marketplace.

Existential R4: Cold Start Failure▼

Consequence: Low liquidity, poor experience, weak retention.
Defense: Create density manually: founder-led acquisition, curated onboarding, concentrated volume.

Consequence: Platform loses revenue and data.
Defense: Make on-platform value superior: credit accumulation, workflow automation, faster payouts, standby access.

Consequence: Erosion of trust and data quality.
Defense: Timestamped uploads, geo checks, merchant verification, pattern analysis, anomaly review. De-emphasize vanity metrics.

05 — Execution Skills Playbook
The 10 core skills required to build Push into a defensible company
1
Merchant Acquisition
Acquire the right initial merchants: cafes, dessert shops, beauty studios, boutique fitness, pop-ups. Strong = motivated, responsive, willing to test repeatedly.
2
Creator Quality Sourcing
Recruit creators who can become reliable commercial operators. Distinguish aesthetic quality from business reliability. Avoid freebie opportunists.
3
Campaign Design
Attractive, measurable, operationally realistic campaigns. One clear goal, simple requirements, verifiable steps, believable payout, reason to repeat.
4
Operational Discipline
Clear milestone states, standard response templates, quick exception identification. Ops feel predictable, fair, visible, low-drama, rule-based.
5
Data Interpretation
Use limited early data intelligently. Separate signal from noise. Measure retention correctly. Track 9 key metrics.
6
Anti-Cliche Positioning
Avoid lazy platform analogies. Frame around outcomes and infrastructure. Explain the moat in operational and economic terms.
7
Retention Architecture
Make platform participation economically compounding. Creators see improved earnings; merchants get faster performance; off-platform feels irrational.
8
Risk Management
Recognize danger early. Identify fraud patterns, weak verticals, creator opportunism, merchant churn risk. Know when a campaign type is structurally bad.
9
Founder Operating Skill
Act as system integrator. Talk to merchants directly. Understand creator psychology. Resist ornamental feature temptation. Mistakes to avoid: building for aesthetics before economics, expanding before density, adding social features too early, confusing signup growth with useful supply.

PUSH Master Strategy

Push is a local creator performance infrastructure layer that helps merchants acquire customers through measurable creator-driven demand.

Core Problem

Merchant Problem

Cannot judge creator quality, compare creators beyond followers, run structured campaigns, or avoid wasting money on low performers.

Creator Problem

Lack pricing power, verifiable performance records, upward mobility, and a stable path from free samples to paid work.

Market Problem

The creator economy has abundant supply, but not structured into a repeatable acquisition channel for local commerce.

5 Strategic Pillars

1. Offer OS: Convert merchant intent into structured campaigns (launch item, drive off-peak traffic, fill event attendance).

2. Creator Credit: Value creators by completion rate, reliability, merchant satisfaction, repeat booking, dispute history.

3. Attribution: Verify via unique codes, check-in proof, merchant confirmation, content verification, redemption evidence.

4. Demand Surface: Real-time demand interface showing where opportunities are, when they close, who qualifies.

5. Liquidity Layer: Standby mechanism for slot fill rate, creator engagement, urgency, merchant confidence.

Company-Building Thesis

The end state is a new market layer for local creator-driven demand: standardize local creator acquisition, rank by performance, route toward better outcomes, compound proprietary data, create lock-in through access, trust, and workflow.

Strategic Red Flags

The company becomes weak if it behaves like: a sample-giveaway app, creator coupon engine, posting board, local discovery map with weak economics, or content engagement platform with shallow verification.

20x Workflow Architecture

The workflow should feel like a controlled economic system, not a manual project-management pipeline.

Design Principles

Fast to understand, hard to misuse, easy to repeat, measurable at every stage, resilient against drop-off and fraud, produces better data over time.

Merchant Workflow (6 Stages)

1. Onboarding: Business info + operational intelligence (demand windows, AOV, high-margin products, capacity).

2. Goal Selection: Drive visits, increase weekday traffic, launch item, boost event attendance, fill underperforming slots.

3. Campaign Structuring: Offer, payout, slots, dates, creator requirements, verification, approval rules.

4. Candidate Routing: Ranked by fulfillment history, local fit, category match, reliability.

5. Execution: Clear deliverables, locked schedule, 7 milestone states.

6. Review & Repeat: Result summary, repeat recommendation, next campaign seed.

7 Milestone States

Accepted → Scheduled → Visited → Proof Submitted → Content Published → Verified → Settled

Creator Workflow (5 Stages)

Onboarding → Opportunity Discovery → Application/Standby → Fulfillment → Settlement & Progression

20x vs Basic Flow

Basic: Post → Apply → Accept → Content → Pay?

20x Push: Goal → Structure → Route → Verify → Settle → Repeat

Workflow Failure Points

Vague instructions, creator ghosting, proof confusion, approval delays, weak fraud controls, low standby response, payout delays, incomplete data.

Pain Points & Root Cause Map

The strongest businesses correctly identify which complaints are structural and build around resolving them.

Merchant Pain Points

A. Cannot Judge Creator Quality: Merchants evaluate by followers and aesthetics (weak signals). Root: no normalized credit layer.

B. Unclear ROI: Cannot answer "did this creator bring real visits?" Root: no structured attribution.

C. Operational Friction: No time for outreach, negotiation, tracking. Root: ad hoc relationship work.

D. Fear of Being Burned: No-shows, low quality, take-and-disappear. Root: no consequence-backed trust.

Creator Pain Points

A. No Pricing Power: Cannot prove worth. Root: no standardized credit system.

B. Income Instability: Random deal flow, low-quality offers. Root: no structured pipeline.

C. Exploitative Low-End: Trapped in "free sample" loops. Root: no distinction between onboarding and growth.

D. Trust Asymmetry: Don't know if merchants pay fairly. Root: weak merchant accountability.

Platform Pain Points

A. Cold Start (two-sided dependency), B. Fraud (weak verification), C. Disintermediation (introduction-only perception), D. Commoditization (under-invest in credit/attribution).

5 Root Causes

Lack of structure, verification, repeatability, durable incentives, and compounding data.

Risk Register & Defense Playbook

Push wins by making the platform more economically rational than off-platform behavior.

Strategic Risks (R1-R3)

R1 Derivative Positioning: Position as infrastructure, not marketplace.

R2 Weak Wedge: Start with one dense area, 1-2 strong verticals.

R3 Feature Drift: Only features improving ROI, progression, defensibility, trust.

Marketplace Risks (R4-R6)

R4 Cold Start: Founder-led density creation.

R5 Low Merchant Retention: Better templates, reporting, creator sorting, fill speed.

R6 Low Creator Retention: Strong progression, limited freebie dependency, clear earnings ladder.

Behavioral Risks (R7-R9)

R7 Disintermediation: Superior on-platform value (credit, automation, payouts).

R8 Opportunist Supply: Gate opportunities behind performance history.

R9 Merchant Abuse: Track merchant behavior and build accountability.

Fraud Risks (R10-R12)

Fake proof, fake redemption, engagement inflation. Defense: timestamped uploads, geo checks, merchant verification, pattern analysis.

Operational Risks (R13-R15)

Approval bottlenecks (SLA nudges), settlement delays (explicit rules), support explosion (exception-based ops).

Competitive (R16)

Large platforms may copy features; defend with vertical depth, workflow, credit layer.

V0.1 Creator Scoring Model

Simple enough for spreadsheets, meaningful enough to predict outcomes, transparent enough for creators, resistant to gaming.

Composite Formula

Push Score = (Completion x 0.30) + (Reliability x 0.20) + (Quality x 0.25) + (Satisfaction x 0.15) + (Engagement x 0.10)

Dimensions

Completion (30%): campaigns completed / accepted. Highest weight because non-completion is worthless to merchants.

Quality (25%): Merchant content rating 1-5.

Reliability (20%): On-time submissions and no-show rate.

Satisfaction (15%): Merchant rebook score 1-5.

Engagement (10%): Post engagement rate (most gameable, lowest weight).

Tier System

New (N/A) → Rising (50+, 3 campaigns) → Reliable (70+, 8 campaigns) → Elite (85+, 20 campaigns). Promotion requires 2 consecutive campaigns at threshold. Demotion has 3-campaign grace period.

Cold Start

First 3 campaigns are probationary. Provisional score starts at 60. Strong early performance triggers fast-track.

Score Decay

Active: no decay. 30-60d inactive: -2/month. 60-90d: -5/month. 90d+: frozen.

Anti-Gaming

Track difficulty distribution, engagement spike analysis, repeated pairing detection, merchant content ratings.
